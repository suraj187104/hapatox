Trust playbook

How we keep HepatoTox honest without drowning you in numbers.

Share this page when someone asks "why should I trust this?"—it lists the routines, safety checks, and human-friendly ways to interpret each model.

Routine we run before every release
  • Blend historical toxic/non-toxic examples so both sides get equal airtime during validation.
  • Shuffle molecules into five scaffold-based folds to mimic real “new chemistry” scenarios.
  • Replay predictions with shuffled labels to ensure the stack is not memorizing.
  • Spot-check explainability heatmaps with chemists before enabling public access.
  • Log every batch job (anonymized) to keep an eye on drift and weird edge cases.
GAHT

Acts like a careful lab partner who would rather over-communicate than miss a red flag.

  • Looks at 3D structure, so bulky groups stand out.
  • Best for "I need the earliest warning" moments.
  • Pair with explainability to justify the caution.
Random Forest

Grounded in classic cheminformatics fingerprints—great when you want something traditional.

  • Comfortable with legacy pipelines and QSAR checks.
  • Feel free to cite it in SOPs or risk memos.
  • Balances sensitivity and calm decision-making.
MLP

Fast checker trained on light-weight descriptors—perfect for triage lists and hackathons.

  • Use it when you care about throughput.
  • Compare its verdict against GAHT to highlight consensus.
  • Great sanity check before sharing with leadership.
Quality practices in plain English
What happens after you click predict
  1. The SMILES is validated and sanitized (no hidden salts).
  2. All three models vote independently so one loud voice cannot dominate.
  3. Consensus logic summarizes the tone (“gentle warning” vs “all clear”).
  4. We attach a plain-language note plus a confidence badge.
  5. Explainability is queued so you can show your team the same view.
When to pause before acting
  • If the molecule contains metals or exotic atoms we do not support yet.
  • When GAHT screams but Random Forest stays calm—double-check with a chemist.
  • After major R&D shifts (new assay, reformulated library) run a mini benchmark first.
How to talk about HepatoTox
  • Lead with the story: “Three models reviewed this molecule and all agreed it looks safe.”
  • Mention the data diet: “Trained on thousands of liver-specific assays, refreshed quarterly.”
  • Invite others to reproduce: “You can upload the same CSV and you will get the identical file back.”
  • Show the explainability heatmap instead of quoting numbers—visuals spark better questions.
What to do if something feels off
  1. Run the molecule again and download the CSV for your notes.
  2. Use the Dataset page to find a similar structure and compare outputs.
  3. Flag the SMILES in your issue tracker so we can add it to the drift watch list.
  4. Share screenshots of the explainability view—those help us diagnose faster than logs.
Our promise

We will never publish raw accuracy numbers on this page—just the habits that keep the system trustworthy.
